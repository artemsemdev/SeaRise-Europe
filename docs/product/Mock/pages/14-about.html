How does this work?

SeaRise Europe combines real climate data from trusted scientific organizations to show you how sea levels might change at any European coastal location. We take the best available research and make it easy to explore.

What we show you

You enter a location anywhere in Europe. We first check if it's near the coast. If it is, we look up scientific sea-level projections for that area and show you how much the water might rise over 10, 20, 30, 50, or 100 years. You can switch between optimistic, moderate, and worst-case forecast models to see a range of possible futures.

Where does the data come from?

Sea-level projections

IPCC AR6

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change Sixth Assessment Report provides global and regional sea-level rise projections under multiple emissions scenarios. This is the gold standard for climate science.

Elevation data

Copernicus DEM

The Copernicus Digital Elevation Model gives us high-resolution terrain data across Europe at 30-meter resolution. We use this to understand which areas sit below projected future sea levels.

Coastline reference

European Environment Agency (EEA)

EEA coastline data helps us determine whether a location is coastal and within the scope of sea-level analysis.

Forecast models explained

Optimistic
NASA (optimistic)

Best case — the world takes strong climate action. Emissions drop fast and temperatures stabilize. Sea levels still rise, but more slowly.

Moderate
Copernicus (moderate)

Middle of the road — some progress on emissions, but not enough to hit the best targets. Sea-level rise is noticeable and ongoing.

Worst case
IPCC (worst case)

If nothing changes — emissions keep rising, ice sheets melt faster, and sea levels rise significantly. The most challenging scenario for coastal communities.

What this is NOT

This is an educational tool based on scientific models. It is not a legal, engineering, insurance, or financial assessment. Do not use it for property decisions, planning permissions, or insurance claims.
  • Does not account for storm surges or extreme wave events
  • Does not include local flood defenses, barriers, or drainage systems
  • Does not model land sinking (subsidence) or uplift
  • Does not include river flooding or rainfall runoff
  • Uses 30-meter satellite elevation data — not building-level precision
  • Projections are based on global models, not hyper-local measurements
