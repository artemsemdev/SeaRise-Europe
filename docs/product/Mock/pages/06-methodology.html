How does this work?

We take real climate data from the world's most trusted scientific organizations and show you what it means for a specific place, in plain language.

1 What we show you

When you search for a location, we check if it's near the European coast. If it is, we show you how much the sea level is expected to rise at that location, based on the time frame and forecast model you pick.

The result is a simple number — like "+0.85 m in 100 years" — with a plain explanation of what it could mean for the area.

2 Where does the data come from?

All data comes from publicly available, peer-reviewed scientific sources:

Sea level forecasts IPCC AR6 Sea Level Projections The world's most comprehensive climate report, published by the United Nations. Provides regional sea-level forecasts under different future scenarios.
Elevation data Copernicus DEM (European Space Agency) Satellite-measured terrain heights at 30-meter resolution. We use this to check how high the ground is relative to sea level.
Coastal risk context European Environment Agency (EEA) European coastal flood indicators that help put the numbers in context.

3 What are the forecast models?

Nobody knows the future exactly, so climate scientists create different scenarios. We label them in simple terms:

NASA (optimistic) Best case — the world takes strong action on climate change. Emissions drop fast. Sea levels still rise, but less.
Copernicus (moderate) Middle of the road — some progress is made, but emissions don't drop as fast as needed. The most commonly referenced scenario.
IPCC (worst case) If nothing changes — emissions keep rising with minimal action. This shows the upper end of what could happen.

4 What this is NOT

This is not a legal, engineering, or financial assessment. It is an educational tool based on scientific models. Don't make property or safety decisions based on this alone.

Other limitations to keep in mind:

• The elevation data is accurate to ~30 meters, not individual buildings.
• We don't include storm surges, tides, or river flooding (yet).
• Local flood defenses are not factored in.
• Some coastal areas may not have full data coverage.
